Ludwig Flamm (1885 - 1964)


Ludwig Flamm was born on 29 January 1885 and died on 4th December 1964. From the oldest son of a clockmaker in Vienna, he became a distinguished member of the Austrian academic community. In 1920 he married Elsa (1891–1965), the youngest of the three daughters of Ludwig Boltzmann. Dieter, one of their four children, was also a theoretical physicist at Vienna University and Christoph, their grand-son is a Professor at the Institute for Theoretical Chemistry of Vienna.


Ludwig Flamm worked in many areas and was considered an “allround-physicist” by his colleagues. He did research on radioactivity and combustion processes, as well as in the areas of world geometry, wave mechanics, wave groups and wave packets.


During his youth, due to an inflammation of the cornea, Ludwig couldn't read, so his mother was reading him the schoolwork. Finally, after many hard years, he passed his final exam with excellent results. After finishing school, he studied physics and mathematics at the University of Vienna under Friedrich Hasenöhrl. As his father died in 1905, Ludwig had to earn his living by giving private lessons.


In 1909 he received his PhD with a dissertation on “Real wave systems”, then became assistant in the Austrian Geodetical Institute. Later, in the port of Pola (since 1918, Pula in Croatia), where he had been sent, he engaged in measurements of astronomical distances. The following year he was at the physics laboratory of the Technical University in Vienna together. He started experimental research on radioactivity, which formed the basis for his later independent theoretical research in this field.


In 1917, Ludwig Flamm published a critical study of the characteristic numbers of proton and electron and their relationship with radiation constants. At the time, it gave the most exact values of fundamental atomic constants providing physicists with a strong base for the calculation of quantum effects.


After obtaining his habilitation in physics at the University of Vienna, he was transferred to the Technical University in Vienna, where he taught and remained until his retirement in 1956. Later on, he became director of the Physical Institute, served as Dean in 1929, and in 1950–1951 as rector of the university.


In 1940 he was elected a full member of the Austrian Academy of Sciences and in 1960 he became an honorary member of the Austrian Physical Society. Three years later (jointly with Karl Przibram) he received the Erwin-Schrödinger prize “in particular for his research in the field of theoretical physics”.
